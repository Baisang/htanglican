What is Anglicanism?

Anglicanism is the third largest Christian tradition in the world after Roman Catholicism and Eastern Orthodoxy, with nearly 80 million members across the globe. The term ‘Anglican’ simply means English—as in the English Church.

An Ancient Church

Christianity arrived in Britain in either the 1st or 2nd century, not long after Christ’s death. Christianity was officially established in England in 597AD through the missionary efforts of Augustine of Canterbury. Anglicanism traces its roots back to this early Christian presence in Britain.

A Reformed Church

During the Protestant Reformation, the Church in England embraced aspects of the Protestant Reformation, including an emphasis on Scripture as the final authority for teaching and preaching, a desire to put the Scriptures and the church’s worship in the common language of the people, a firm commitment to the doctrine of justification by faith, and an evangelical and missionary zeal to spread the Gospel to all peoples.

A Church with Catholic Heritage

Though the Reformation separated Anglicanism from Roman Catholicism and the immediate oversight of the Pope, Anglicanism maintained a catholic heritage, including the threefold office of ministers in bishops, priests, and deacons in succession with the first apostles, an emphasis on the teachings of the Church Fathers especially as they are expressed in the Apostle’s, Nicene, and Athanasian Creeds, and the beauty of liturgical and sacramental worship.

A Global Church

Because Anglicanism originated in the Church of England, Anglicanism spread wherever the British Empire spread. Today there are Anglican churches in as diverse places as the Americas (North and South), Africa, the Middle-East, and Southeast Asia. Anglicans, though globally diverse, stand united in their common worship of God in Jesus Christ.

A Prayerful Church

One of the main desires of the English Reformers (especially Thomas Cranmer) was to put the prayers and worship of the church in the peoples’ native language. The product of this desire was the Book of Common Prayer. With roughly 80% of its text coming straight from the Bible, the Book of Common Prayer gives every Christian the ability to pray Scripturally-rooted prayers in their own language. Thomas Cranmer and the other English Reformers wanted each Christian home to be able to pray like the monks in their monasteries. The Book of Common Prayer puts all of life to prayer—from the beginning of the day to the close of the day, from birth to death. Many of these beautiful prayers have even influenced wider culture—like the marriage and funeral liturgies. Thomas Cranmer and other English Reformers paid for this great treasure with their lives.

A Spirit-filled Church

Anglicans believe that the Spirit is at work in the Church, teaching, healing, pouring out spiritual gifts, leading the Church, and calling the lost back to God just as He was in the early church. In a way, this means that Anglicans are charismatic Christians—we believe that the Spirit is still very much at work among us.

A Sacramental Church

Anglicans believe that God delights in revealing Himself and pouring out His grace through concrete physical signs that we can touch, feel, and see, just as God did in the Incarnation of Jesus Christ. We believe that God makes this grace available through the waters of Baptism and as we celebrate Holy Communion together. Every Sunday Anglicans come to our Lord’s Table as Jesus makes himself truly present to us in our worship in order to nourish us as we pour out our lives, just as He did, for the life of the world.
